Methodology

Data & processing pipeline

Eight steps, from raw NetCDF reanalysis fields to a bivariate population-exposure map. Each step lists the workflow and the practical notes recorded along the way.

Datasets

Three open geospatial sources

CAMS European Air Quality Reanalysis

Retrospective air-quality modelling combining 11 regional chemistry-transport models (the ENSEMBLE median). Hourly pollutant fields delivered as NetCDF.

NO₂PM2.5PM10 2021 & 2023μg/m³

ESRI 10 m Annual Land Cover

High-resolution annual land cover from the ArcGIS Living Atlas, used to detect transitions between 2021 and 2023.

10 m2021 & 2023

WorldPop — Population Counts

Gridded 2023 population counts for Romania, used for the exposure assessment.

2023Population count

FAO GAUL Level 2

Administrative boundaries used to aggregate population and pollution classes for the bivariate map.

Admin L2Vector
Note · NetCDF A file format for multi-dimensional scientific data (time, latitude, longitude, height). CAMS fields are self-describing — variable, dimensions and metadata such as units and source travel with the data — which makes them ideal for analysis in QGIS, Python (xarray) and Panoply.
Processing

The eight-step pipeline

01

Monthly aggregated maps — December 2023

Monthly mean aggregation simplifies analysis of the pollution data. For December 2023:

  1. Download the CAMS European Air Quality Reanalysis (2023 hourly mesh dataset).
  2. Assign projection EPSG:4326 · WGS 84 to avoid compatibility issues.
  3. Use the mesh calculator to average from Dec 1 to Dec 31.
  4. Rasterize the resulting mesh for later annual aggregation.
  5. Clip the raster to the country extent.
Comment Pixel-grid compatibility with the already-existing earlier months mattered. An extra Raster Tools → Align Rasters step used one of the previous months as the reference layer.
02

Yearly aggregated maps — 2021 & 2023

A yearly mean across the twelve monthly rasters produces the annual layers, computed with r.series (aggregation = mean).

Comment The comparison showed a downward trend from 2021 to 2023.
03

Concentration maps per pollutant — 2023

The EU sets annual-mean limits per pollutant, and concentration is reported on five levels. Binning follows the EEA ranges via Processing Toolbox → Raster analysis → Reclassify by table.

Comment The colour-coded result tracked population centres, with lower values over forested zones.
04

Spatio-temporal trend analysis (AMAC)

Change between 2021 and 2023 is quantified as the annual-mean difference, enabling change detection, hotspot identification and later correlation analysis.

  1. Raster calculator: average POLLUTANT 2023 − average POLLUTANT 2021.
  2. Apply discrete symbology to the rate of change:
  • > 0 μg/m³ — pollution increase · "environmental risk zones."
  • < 0 μg/m³ — pollution decrease · "improvement zones."
  • ≈ 0 μg/m³ — atmospheric stability · no significant change.
Comment Overall stability across the country, with a slight decrease in pollutant concentrations. These AMAC maps are the minimum required WebGIS layer.
05

Land cover change map & statistics — 2021→2023

Land cover change (LCC) maps summarise how classes shift over time, allowing persistence, sources of gain and sources of loss to be calculated per class.

  • Download ESRI 10 m Land Cover for 2021 and 2023 (Romania).
  • Reproject to EPSG:4326; build virtual rasters from the tiles; clip to boundaries.
  • Compute LCC with ("LC_2021" × 100) + "LC_2023".
  • Per class of interest — Built Area (NO₂), Trees (PM10), Crops (PM2.5) — compute persistence, top-3 sources of gain and top-3 destinations of loss (pixelwise).
  • Bonus: report area percentage, not just pixel percentage.
Comment To report true area, rasters were reprojected to Pulkovo 1942 before computing per-class areas — minimising distortion.
06

Pollution correlation & zonal statistics

A first assessment of possible links between pollution change and land cover change, across three groupings (Stable · Gain · Loss).

  • Resample CAMS change maps to ESRI's 10 m resolution.
  • Build a binary change map (zone values) per studied class.
  • Vectorize → dissolve by zone value.
  • Compute zonal statistics (mean, min, max) using the pollutant net-change map.
  • Produce a bar chart of mean/min/max per zone.
Comment · the dissolve bottleneck The dissolve of millions of disjoint polygons was the computational bottleneck: single-threaded QGIS took 24+ hours (some runs cancelled past 30 h at ~50%). ArcGIS completed the same dissolve in ~40 minutes, so the team ran each part in whichever software was more efficient, fixing geometries in QGIS before the final zonal-statistics step.
07

Bivariate population-exposure map

A bivariate map relates population density and pollution values per administrative region.

  • Download WorldPop population counts; reset no-data to 0.
  • Classify population into 5 classes (0 + 4 quantiles).
  • Load FAO GAUL Level 2 for Romania.
  • Compute the median population class per region and the max pollution value per region.
  • Bivariate class = (pol_class_max × 10) + pop_class_median; style and visualise.
08

Population exposure — pie chart

The share of the population exposed to each pollution level, per pollutant.

  • Dissolve the Step-7 layer by pol_class_max.
  • Sum population per dissolved polygon (per pollutant).
  • Build a pie chart with DataPlotly.
