Processing

The eight-step pipeline

01

Monthly aggregated maps for December 2023

Monthly mean aggregation simplifies analysis of the pollution data. For December 2023, the procedure consisted in:

  1. Download the CAMS European Air Quality Reanalysis (2023 hourly mesh dataset).
  2. Assign projection EPSG:4326 · WGS 84 to avoid compatibility issues.
  3. Use the mesh calculator to average from Dec 1 to Dec 31.
  4. Rasterize the resulting mesh for later annual aggregation.
  5. Clip the raster to the country extent.
Comment Pixel-grid compatibility with the already-existing earlier months mattered. An extra Raster Tools → Align Rasters step used one of the previous months as the reference layer.
02

Yearly aggregated maps for 2021 & 2023

A yearly mean across the twelve monthly rasters produces the annual layers, computed with r.series (aggregation = mean).

03

Concentration maps per pollutant for 2023

The EU sets annual-mean limits per pollutant, and concentration is reported on five levels. Binning follows the EEA ranges via Processing Toolbox → Raster analysis → Reclassify by table.

04

Spatio-temporal trend analysis (AMAC)

Change between 2021 and 2023 is quantified as the annual-mean difference, enabling change detection, hotspot identification and later correlation analysis.

  1. Raster calculator: average POLLUTANT 2023 − average POLLUTANT 2021.
  2. Apply discrete symbology to the rate of change:
  • > 0 μg/m³ — pollution increase · "environmental risk zones."
  • < 0 μg/m³ — pollution decrease · "improvement zones."
  • ≈ 0 μg/m³ — atmospheric stability · no significant change.
Comment Overall stability across the country, with a slight decrease in pollutant concentrations. These AMAC maps are the minimum required WebGIS layer.
05

Land cover change map & statistics — 2021→2023

Land cover change (LCC) maps provide a visual representation of land cover transitions between two points in time. In addition to visualizing these changes, they enable the calculation of metrics such as persistence, sources of gain, and sources of loss for each land cover class, providing a more detailed understanding of the observed transitions. The LCC maps and the associated statistics were generated using the following workflow:

  • Download ESRI 10 m Land Cover for 2021 and 2023 (Romania).
  • Reproject to EPSG:4326, build virtual rasters from the tiles and clip to boundaries.
  • Compute LCC with ("LC_2021" × 100) + "LC_2023".
  • Per class of interest (Built Area (NO₂), Trees (PM10) and Crops (PM2.5)), compute persistence, top-3 sources of gain and top-3 destinations of loss (pixelwise).
  • Bonus: report area percentage, not just pixel percentage.
Comment To report true area, rasters were reprojected to Pulkovo 1942/Stereo 70 before computing per-class areas, minimizing distortion.
06

Pollution correlation & zonal statistics

Assessing the relationship between changes in pollutant concentrations and the three land cover transition groups (Stable, Gain, and Loss) provides a first indication of possible links between air pollution and land cover dynamics. While this analysis does not compute a statistical correlation, the zonal statistics allow the pollutant concentration changes to be compared across the three transition groups. The analysis was carried out as follows:

  • Resample CAMS change maps to ESRI's 10 m resolution.
  • Build a binary change map (zone values) per studied class.
  • Vectorize and dissolve by zone value.
  • Compute zonal statistics (mean, min, max) using the pollutant net-change map.
  • Produce a bar chart of mean/min/max per zone.
Comment the dissolve bottleneck This stage was by far the most computationally demanding part of the laboratory. The dissolve operation proved to be the computational bottleneck. In QGIS, the process required more than 24 hours on the fastest machine available to the group, while other attempts were cancelled after more than 30 hours with the operation still incomplete. The extremely large number of polygons generated during the polygonization step made the dissolve operation prohibitively slow.

To overcome this limitation, the dissolve step was also tested in ArcGIS. Under the same conditions, ArcGIS completed the operation in approximately 40 minutes, producing equivalent results. Consequently, both QGIS and ArcGIS were used during this stage of the project, selecting the software that offered the most efficient implementation for each task.

Using multiple GIS platforms introduced minor geometry inconsistencies. These were resolved by applying the Fix Geometries tool in QGIS before computing the final zonal statistics
07

Bivariate population exposure map

Bivariate maps provide a visual representation of the spatial relationship between two variables, allowing areas with similar or contrasting patterns to be identified. In this step, a bivariate map was produced to compare population density and pollutant concentrations across Romania, providing an initial visual assessment of their spatial association. The bivariate map was generated in the following way:

  • Download WorldPop population counts and reset no-data to 0.
  • Classify population into 5 classes (0 + 4 quantiles).
  • Load FAO GAUL Level 2 for Romania.
  • Compute the median population class per region and the max pollution value per region.
  • Compute the bivariate class = (pol_class_max × 10) + pop_class_median.
  • Apply the style and visualize the bivariate maps.
08

Population exposure (pie chart)

TThe final step focuses on assessing the proportion of the population exposed to different pollutant concentration ranges for each pollutant. To effectively communicate these results, pie charts were generated to illustrate the percentage of the population falling within each concentration class.

  • Dissolve the Step-7 layer by pol_class_max.
  • Compute the sum of the population in each dissolved polygon (pollution level) for each pollutant.
  • Build a pie chart with DataPlotly.
